// 01 – The Problem

The radio you're using
is already compromised.

Most people don't know how their walkie-talkie actually works. Here's the short version: it doesn't transmit directly to the other person. It transmits to a server. Here's what that means for you.

THREAT 01
Production Orders

Under the Canadian Criminal Code, police can obtain a production order – a court document that forces a company to hand over your data. If your voice traffic routes through a vendor's server, that vendor gets served, not you. You never find out. The conversation is already gone.

THREAT 02
Chinese OEM Radios

That $290 walkie-talkie on Amazon with a pre-installed SIM? It was manufactured in Shenzhen. Under China's National Intelligence Law, any Chinese company can be legally compelled to hand over user data to the state – with no court order, no notice, and no appeal. You are registering your device with that company every time you key up.

THREAT 03
SIM & IMEI Tracking

Every cellular device has an IMEI – a permanent hardware ID. The SIM inside your radio has an IMSI. Both are logged by carrier towers the moment you power on, whether you're transmitting or not. Law enforcement can request your location history, call logs, and group associations from the carrier without ever touching your device.

THREAT 04
Vendor-Controlled Servers

Popular PTT apps like Zello route everything through their own servers. In December 2024, Zello suffered a confirmed data breach. More importantly: any US or Canadian company can be compelled to produce call records, group membership, location logs, and in some cases, recordings – through standard civil and criminal legal process.

THREAT 05
Encryption You Can't Verify

Vendors advertise "AES-256 encryption." What they don't tell you: they hold the keys. End-to-end encryption where the provider manages keys is mathematically equivalent to no encryption from a legal-process standpoint. A court order to the provider is a court order to decrypt.

THREAT 06
You Don't Know It's Happening

Production orders, carrier requests, and foreign government data access are typically sealed. There is no legal requirement to notify you that your communications were accessed. By the time you find out – if you find out – the information has already been used.

// 03 – Why This Matters Legally

The production order
problem, explained.

Under the Canadian Criminal Code s. 487.014, police can obtain a production order – a court document requiring a company to hand over specific data. The target of that order is the entity that holds the data.

If you use Zello, the order goes to Zello. If you use a Global PTT radio, the order goes to a Chinese company that has no obligation under Canadian law and full obligation under Chinese law to cooperate with Beijing. Either way, you are not notified. You have no standing to challenge it. The data is produced.

With ClearChannel, you are the data holder. A production order would have to name you personally, served to you, giving you full Charter s.8 rights, legal standing to challenge, and – because we configure zero retention – potentially nothing to produce even if the order succeeds.

This is not a loophole. This is how privacy is supposed to work.
